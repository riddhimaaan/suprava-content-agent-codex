Close (born out of ElasticSales) is one of those rare SaaS companies where the founder turned “selling” into the product, the marketing channel, and the brand. The story isn’t a clever template; it’s the compounding advantage of doing unscalable work (calls, demos, learning loops, and content) long enough that it becomes unfair.

---

# Cold Email / Outbound: When “Relentless Follow-Up” Was the Product (Close / Steli Efti)

Cold email used to be a copy game. In 2026 it’s mostly a trust game: inboxes are saturated, “personalization” is commoditized, and people can smell automation a mile away. Close’s early story is a clean counterexample: the edge came from founder-led hustle, tight feedback loops with customers, and a refusal to treat follow-up as optional.

---

## ElasticSales: they pre-sold an outsourced sales service by calling funded startups (with a “refund any time” guarantee)

Before Close was software, Steli Efti was selling as a service. The ElasticSales origin story is basically: pick a market you understand (startups), compress time (two weeks to validate), and reduce risk so far that buyers can say “yes” fast (a strong money-back guarantee). Then he pulled lists of funded companies (e.g., via Crunchbase), and did the unglamorous work of calling them to pre-sell the service and prove demand existed.

Source: GrowthRamp profile of Steli Efti / ElasticSales origin story https://www.growthramp.io/articles/steli-efti

---

## “We were disappointed by most sales software”… so they built the tool they needed, then shipped Close.io in Jan 2013

One of the sharper “founder logic” moments in Close’s materials is how bluntly they frame it: existing CRMs made salespeople into data-entry clerks, and ElasticSales needed something built for selling velocity, not record-keeping. So they started building an internal tool in 2012, and released Close.io publicly in January 2013 — a classic “scratch your own itch” move where the operating constraint (selling a lot, fast) becomes product requirements.

Source: Close resource “SaaS Sales for Startup Founders” (PDF) https://resource-downloads.close.com/resources/saas-sales.pdf

---

## Their unfair advantage: selling for 200+ venture-backed startups before they ever sold their own product

In a long Indie Hackers interview, Steli describes how ElasticSales had sold for “over 200 venture-backed startups” in ~18 months. That detail matters because it explains why Close could be opinionated early: they’d already seen patterns across dozens of funnels, objections, and buying motions. The meta-story is: they weren’t learning sales on their own product from scratch; they arrived with a dataset of real-world selling scars.

Source: Indie Hackers podcast transcript https://www.indiehackers.com/podcast/053-steli-efti-of-close-io

---

## A contrarian early filter: knowing who your customer isn’t (and resisting the “big logo” trap)

In the same interview, Steli tells a story about being approached early by a much bigger “unicorn-type” company. The temptation is obvious: big logo, big deal, big validation. But his point is that startups routinely underestimate the true cost of acquiring and supporting a customer segment they’re not built for; the mistake isn’t just “harder sales,” it’s bending product, roadmap, and support around the wrong buyer.

Source: Indie Hackers podcast transcript https://www.indiehackers.com/podcast/053-steli-efti-of-close-io

---

## Founder-led content as distribution: they found the “sales for startups” niche when nobody was writing about it

One repeatable Close moment that’s easy to overlook: they treated writing and teaching as the top-of-funnel, not a nice-to-have. Steli’s own “journey” writeups emphasize that “nobody in Silicon Valley was writing about sales,” so Close claimed that territory and built authority that converted into inbound. This is less “content marketing” and more “own the conversation your buyers are already having privately.”

Source: Startup Founder Stories profile (and its links back to Close’s own blog) https://startupfounderstories.com/stories/steli-efti-close-sales-content

---

## “Talk to your customers” isn’t a platitude in their world — it’s the mechanism that makes follow-up feel human

Close’s “Talk to Your Customers” book is essentially an argument that the fastest competitive advantage in B2B isn’t a feature; it’s learning loops. When you actually talk to customers (not just email sequences), your follow-ups stop feeling like “touchpoints” and start feeling like a continuation of a real conversation — which is exactly what most cold email fails to do.

Source: Close resource “Talk to Your Customers” (PDF) https://resource-downloads.close.com/resources/talk-to-your-customers.pdf
