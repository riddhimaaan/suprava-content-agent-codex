# Content Themes — What Suprava's Content Is About

This file tells the research agent what kind of stories to look for and what to ignore. Read this before running any Mode 3 searches.

---

## What we're looking for

The content narrative is shifting away from AI tools and automation, toward the human, creative, and often offline things that actually work in the real world right now. The audience is founders, marketers, and operators who are tired of the same playbooks and want to see what's actually happening out there.

### The themes — in priority order

**1. Making tech fun again**
Brands that use animation, humor, cinematic storytelling, or creative characters to tell their story — instead of product demos and feature lists. Think Silicon Mania doing Simpsons-style animated narratives of the Elon vs Sam Altman saga. Or a SaaS company that hired a filmmaker instead of a marketer. Anything where a tech brand is genuinely entertaining, not just educational.

**2. Historical founder stories that still land today**
How Airbnb's founders got their first users. How Bezos convinced his first investors. The cold email Paul Graham almost didn't read. Stories from 2005–2015 where founders did something scrappy and creative that worked — and that most people have either forgotten or never heard. These aren't "listicle facts" — they're the full story with context, what was at stake, what they actually did, how it played out.

**3. Human-touch in the age of AI**
Companies deliberately choosing to be less automated and more human — handwritten notes, physical packages, personal video messages, things that take actual effort — specifically because AI has made everything else feel automated and cold. The contrast is the story: in a world where everyone's inbox is flooded with AI-written emails, a handwritten doormat from a startup lands like a thunderbolt.

**4. The taste economy**
Brands and founders who are winning not because of budget or distribution, but because their work is genuinely good — visually, narratively, experientially. The idea that "taste" is becoming the real competitive advantage. Real examples: a small agency that went viral because their deck was beautiful. A founder whose thread blew up because the writing was actually good. A product launch that got shared because it was clever, not because it was boosted.

**5. Creative launch strategies**
Service companies, agencies, and non-SaaS businesses that launched like a startup and booked hundreds of demos. Launch strategists emerging as one of the highest-paying roles in B2B. The idea that a launch isn't just for product companies — and that the companies doing this right are not the ones anyone expects. Real named examples of campaigns, what they did, how many demos they booked, what made it work.

**6. Physical and offline GTM**
Specific campaigns where brands used physical, offline tactics to acquire customers or build brand. Physical mail clubs where people pay to receive handwritten brand notes monthly. Startups sending doormats, Apple Watches, or custom gifts to target accounts. Run clubs, book clubs, sports events, and library meetups used as community-led growth channels. The more specific and named the better — "a startup in SF did X and got Y result" is what we want.

**7. Unconventional storytelling formats**
Any brand doing something genuinely different with how they communicate — not just what they say but how they say it. A B2B company that writes like a novelist. A founder who shares weekly updates that read like a journal. A brand that runs a narrative arc across 12 posts like a TV season. The format itself is the story.

---

## What we are NOT looking for

Reject any result that falls into these categories — even if the writing is good:

- Tool stacks, tech stack recommendations, software comparisons
- AI SDR, outbound automation, sales automation playbooks
- Standard GTM frameworks ("here's the PLG vs. SLG breakdown")
- Generic cold email advice ("personalize your subject line", "keep it under 3 sentences")
- Anything that starts with "here are N ways to..."
- Content about AI capabilities, model comparisons, or what AI can do for marketers
- Anything Suprava has already covered heavily: LinkedIn content strategy, Instagram Reels for B2B, AISDR, standard outbound sequences

The test: would a founder who's been in growth for 5+ years read this and learn something they didn't know? If not, it's not the right story.

## Seed examples — use these to find more like them

These are known stories that already fit the brief. Use them as starting points when searching — find the full story, then look for similar ones:

- **Delph** — sent custom doormats to their top target accounts instead of cold emails
- **A startup (name unknown)** — sent Apple Watches to 100 startups in SF/NY as an outreach campaign; reportedly got massive response rates
- **Physical mail clubs** — brands charging a monthly subscription fee for handwritten physical notes about their product/brand; people are paying for this
- **Silicon Mania** — X/Twitter account creating animated, Simpsons-style narrative content about tech industry stories (Elon vs Sam, etc.) — massive engagement
- **Launch strategists** — the emergence of this as a job title and why it's now one of the highest-paying roles in B2B marketing
- **Run clubs as GTM** — specific brands or agencies that started run clubs as a community-led way to build pipeline without a single cold email

When you find a story from these seeds, also look for 2–3 similar stories in the same category. One good seed usually means there are 5 more like it.
