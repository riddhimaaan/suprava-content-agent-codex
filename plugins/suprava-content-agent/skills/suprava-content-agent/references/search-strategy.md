# Search Strategy — How to Find the Right Stories

This file guides how to search. Read it before running searches in Mode 3 (and optionally in Modes 1 and 2 for better results).

---

## The core principle

Search for stories, not advice.

The internet is full of articles telling people what to do. What it has far less of — and what Suprava's audience actually wants — is what specific people and brands actually did, and what happened. The research agent's job is to find those specific instances.

A good result looks like: "Delph sent handmade doormats to their 10 highest-priority target accounts and got 7 meetings booked in one week."

A bad result looks like: "10 creative ways to stand out in outbound sales."

---

## Where to look

Not all sources are equal. Prioritize in this order:

**Tier 1 — Where the story actually lives:**
- X (Twitter) posts from the founders or company accounts themselves
- LinkedIn posts from founders, marketers, or operators sharing what they did
- Personal newsletters or blogs from practitioners (not agencies selling services)
- Startup podcast transcripts and interviews where founders tell their story directly
- Product Hunt launch pages and the discussions around them

**Tier 2 — Where good case studies sometimes live:**
- Niche B2B media (The Hustle, First Round Review, Lenny's Newsletter, Demand Curve)
- Startup accelerator blogs (YC, a16z, Sequoia) when they feature specific founder stories
- Reddit threads (r/entrepreneur, r/startups) where someone shares a real experience

**Tier 3 — Use with caution:**
- General marketing blogs — only useful if they contain a specific named case study with real numbers
- News articles — good for facts and context, but rarely tell the full story

**Avoid entirely:**
- Generic SEO content farms (anything ranking for "best GTM strategies 2024")
- Content by agencies pitching their own services
- Generic listicles without named examples

---

## What a good result looks like

Before including anything in the output, check:

- Is there a real company or person named? (Not "a startup in the Bay Area")
- Is there a specific action described? (Not "they got creative with their outreach")
- Is there a measurable or observable outcome? (Not "it worked really well")
- Can the source be traced? (A tweet, a post, an article — something linkable)

If you can answer yes to all four: it's a good result.
If you can only answer yes to one or two: keep searching.

---

## How to approach different topics

**For GTM and acquisition stories:**
Look for: specific campaign names, founder posts about what they tried, before/after comparisons, stories that got shared widely because they were surprising or different.

**For cold email and outreach stories:**
Look for: historical founder stories (pre-2015 especially), stories about unusual outreach tactics that worked, the specific message that got a response from an investor or customer.

**For content and storytelling stories:**
Look for: creators or brands that went from obscure to well-known through a specific piece of content or format, the backstory of a viral campaign, founder threads that changed how people think about a topic.

**For offline and physical GTM stories:**
Look for: specific gifts or packages sent and the response they got, physical events used as pipeline generation, any brand treating physical touchpoints as a primary channel rather than a novelty.

---

## When searches aren't working

If the first few searches return only generic content:

1. Add a founder or company name you already know (from content-themes.md seed examples) to anchor the search
2. Search for the outcome rather than the tactic ("booked 40 demos from a launch post" rather than "creative launch strategy")
3. Search for the category of story you want, with a time filter (2023, 2024, 2025)
4. Try searching X/Twitter directly for the topic + "thread" — practitioners often share stories in thread format
5. If you find one good story, look for who shared it and what else they've written — one good source often leads to more

If after 8 searches you still have fewer than 5 angles: be transparent in the output about which areas had strong stories and which were harder to find, and suggest Suprava share any stories she's seen so you can research the full context.
