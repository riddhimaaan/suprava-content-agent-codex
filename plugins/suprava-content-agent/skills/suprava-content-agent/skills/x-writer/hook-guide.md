# X Hook Guide

Use this only for X posts and threads.

The hook has one job: make the reader feel they need the next line now.

## Hook Standard

A strong X hook should feel:
- bold
- clear
- a little tense
- easy to understand in one glance
- specific enough to feel real

The reader should not need background knowledge to care. If the hook names a company, person, trend, or tool, add the missing context in plain words fast.

Bad: "Delph changed outbound forever."
Better: "A tiny AI startup booked meetings by mailing doormats to buyers."

Bad: "A new GTM playbook is emerging."
Better: "Cold email is getting ignored, so some startups are going back to physical mail."

## Dopamine Rules

Use at least one of these in every hook:

1. Shock
   Show a result or move that feels unusual.
   Example: "A startup got sales meetings from custom doormats."

2. Tension
   Put two ideas in conflict.
   Example: "Everyone is chasing AI outbound. One team won with old-school mail."

3. Specific payoff
   Promise the exact thing the reader will learn.
   Example: "This is the simple offline trick that got buyers to reply."

4. Curiosity gap
   Give enough detail to pull them in, but hold back the reveal.
   Example: "The smartest cold email campaign I saw this year used zero emails."

5. Status flip
   Show a small player beating a bigger or louder system.
   Example: "A small startup made enterprise buyers pay attention without a bigger ad budget."

## Hook Patterns

### Bold claim

Use when the story has a strong opinion or clear lesson.

Formula:
`[Common belief] is wrong. [Sharper truth].`

Examples:
- "Cold email is not dead. Lazy cold email is."
- "AI did not make outreach easier. It made boring outreach easier to ignore."

### Strange proof

Use when the story has an odd tactic or surprising proof point.

Formula:
`[Company/person] did [unexpected action]. It worked because [simple reason].`

Examples:
- "A startup mailed doormats to prospects. It worked because nobody ignores their own front door."
- "A founder sold software with handwritten notes. The tool was new, but the trust was old."

### Context gap

Use when the reader may not know the company or topic.

Formula:
`[Simple context]. Then [surprising move].`

Examples:
- "Delph sells AI tools for sales teams. To get attention, they used doormats."
- "Most buyers ignore cold emails now. That made one offline campaign stand out even more."

### Pain first

Use when the story starts from a clear reader problem.

Formula:
`If [pain], [story/solution] will matter to you.`

Examples:
- "If buyers keep ignoring your emails, this offline campaign is worth studying."
- "If your best leads never reply, the problem may not be your copy."

### Result first

Use when the story has a number or clear outcome.

Formula:
`[Result]. The surprising part: [how it happened].`

Examples:
- "7 meetings in one week. The surprising part: they came from doormats, not emails."
- "One small campaign got enterprise buyers to reply. It started with physical mail."

### Contrarian

Use when the story pushes against common advice.

Formula:
`Stop [common action]. Start [sharper action].`

Examples:
- "Stop sending more cold emails. Make fewer buyers feel impossible to ignore."
- "Stop trying to sound scalable. Sometimes the unscalable thing wins."

## Bad Hooks To Avoid

Do not use:
- "Most people..."
- "Everyone is talking about..."
- "Here's what you need to know..."
- "Let's talk about..."
- "In today's world..."
- "The future of..."
- "This is a game-changer..."
- vague hype like "insane", "wild", or "crazy" without proof

## Hook Checklist

Before finalizing, ask:
- Can a stranger understand the hook without knowing the company?
- Is the wording simple enough for a tenth grader?
- Does the first line create tension, curiosity, or a clear payoff?
- Is there a concrete name, result, action, or problem?
- Can any word be simpler?
- Does it make the next line feel necessary?
