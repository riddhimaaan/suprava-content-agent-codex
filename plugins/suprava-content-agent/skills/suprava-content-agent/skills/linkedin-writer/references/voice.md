# Suprava's LinkedIn Voice

This document defines how Suprava writes on LinkedIn — her structural patterns, signature phrases, formatting habits, and tonal markers. Derived from analysis of her 27 published templates.

**Important:** This captures *how* she writes, not *what* she writes about. Her content themes are evolving (see `references/content-themes.md` in the root). The voice stays consistent regardless of topic.

---

## Who she sounds like

A practitioner who has done the work and is sharing what she found — not a thought leader, not a coach, not a marketer performing expertise. Direct without being blunt. Tactical without being dry. She sounds like someone talking to a colleague, not presenting to an audience.

She is never vague. She does not hedge. She does not build up to the point — she opens with it.

---

## Hook patterns

She uses a small set of hook types. Pick one per post. Do not mix them.

**Outcome hook** — Opens with a specific result, then immediately offers to show how.

> Get 100+ emails in less than 10 mins using LinkedIn

> How I get 80%+ open rate and a 20% reply rate?
> In my cold-email campaigns…

Pattern: Number + outcome + timeframe or context. The specificity does the work.

---

**Problem/accusation hook** — Opens by calling out something the reader is doing wrong.

> You get 0 responses from your cold-email campaign because..
> You are still relying on Apollo.io's data!

> You are losing leads because ❌
> You do pitch slap

Pattern: "You are [doing wrong thing] because [reason]" or "You are losing [thing] because [behavior]". The ❌ emoji is part of this hook type — it signals "stop, this is wrong."

---

**Command hook** — Opens with a direct instruction, usually "Stop [doing X]."

> Stop getting ghosted by your leads ❌
> Stop using sales navigator! ❌
> Stop spending $5000/mo on lead-gen agencies

Pattern: "Stop [action]" or "Stop [spending/waiting/doing]". Often followed by a short hook line before pivoting to the solution.

---

**Steal/reveal hook** — Opens with a giveaway or secret.

> STEAL MY STRATEGY!
> Lead-gen agencies are hiding this secret from you!
> Steal my email copy

Pattern: All-caps or bold emphasis on the give. Implies the reader is getting something others don't have.

---

**Personal story hook** — Opens with a specific personal stat or duration.

> I sent 100 cold DMs every day for 17 months.
> Here's what I learned:

Pattern: "I [did specific thing] for [specific duration/volume]. Here's what I learned:" — the colon matters, it creates a hard stop before the list.

---

**Promise hook** — Opens with a numbered outcome and a strong guarantee.

> These 5 agency hacks will 3x your revenue in 90 days. (GURANTEED)
> (The last one is the best tip)

Pattern: Number + outcome + timeframe + parenthetical teaser. The second line (in parentheses) is optional but characteristic.

## Body structure

**Paragraph length:** 1–3 lines per block, then a line break. Never a wall of text. If a paragraph runs longer than 3 lines, break it.

**Step-by-step content:** Numbered with bold step numbers or emoji ordinals (1️⃣, 2️⃣). Each step gets its own short block.

> 1. Go to LinkedIn jobs
> 2. Search for your niche with a "remote" filter
> 3. Scrape the leads list using Lix

Or for more detailed steps, the number gets its own line, the explanation follows:

> 1. Target from job platforms:
>
> Why?
>
> Because the prospect who is posting about the job...

**Bullet lists:** Use dashes (`-`) or arrows (`→`, `➡`). Never mixed in the same list.

**"Why?" sub-blocks:** For longer posts, after stating a tactic she adds a short "Why?" block that explains the reasoning. This is one of her most recognizable structural patterns.

> 2. Scrape event participants:
>
> Why?
>
> Let's make you understand this by an example...

## Signature phrases and transitions

These are patterns that appear across multiple posts. Use them to sound like her, not to mechanically insert them everywhere.

**"The best part?"** — Used to introduce the biggest value point, usually before a price reveal, an unexpected outcome, or a counterintuitive fact. Appears in its own one-line block.

> The best part?
>
> I'm giving this system to one of you for absolutely FREE.

---

**"Now..."** — A one-word transition paragraph. Used to shift from problem to solution, or from setup to action.

> Now...
>
> If you think this is interesting, let me know in the comments

---

**"Means,"** — A connecting fragment used to explain a consequence. One of her more distinctive ticks.

> Means,
>
> The prospects you are reaching out to are getting the same msgs EVERY SINGLE DAY!!

---

**"Here's how 👇"** / **"Here's what you need to do:"** — Introduces a step-by-step list. The emoji version is more casual; the colon version is for more instructional content.

---

**"And that's it!"** — Casual close for step-by-step posts. Signals the process is complete before moving to the CTA.

---

**"Not gonna lie..."** — Used sparingly for an honest admission or a mild vulnerability.

---

**"Do this instead 👇"** — Follows a "you're doing this wrong" hook. Signals the pivot from problem to solution.

## Numbers and specificity

Suprava always uses specific numbers. Never approximate.

- Not "many responses" → "20% reply rate"
- Not "high response" → "7 of the 10 booked a call"
- Not "affordable" → "$49/mo"
- Not "significant results" → "22+ leads in less than 12 hrs"

Numbers carry the credibility. When writing in her voice, if there's a number available, use it.

## CTA patterns

She uses a small set of CTA structures. Pick one per post. Don't stack multiple CTAs in the same post.

**Comment-bait (most common):**

> Like
> Comment "SaaS"
>
> And I will send it your way!

The three-step pattern: Like → Comment [keyword] → DM. Always a specific keyword in quotes. Always a clear exchange of value.

---

**Question CTA:**

> How do you find emails from LinkedIn?

A direct question to the reader, placed at the end. No ask — just an invitation to engage. Used for posts that share information rather than giveaways.

---

**Service pitch CTA:**

> If you need my help setting up this lead gen funnel for you DM me and let's talk!

Direct and short. One line. Often preceded by a separator line (`----`).

---

**Follow CTA (usually appended to another CTA):**

> Follow Suprava for more content on lead-gen!

She refers to herself in the third person in follow CTAs. This is consistent across posts.

---

**Separator before CTA:** She almost always adds a `----` or `---` line before the final CTA block to visually separate content from the ask.

## Visual formatting

**Emojis as punctuation, not decoration:**
- ❌ = "this is wrong / stop doing this" (in hook or alongside a negative point)
- ✅ = "this is right / do this" (in positive lists or value propositions)
- 👇 = "keep reading / scroll down"
- → and ➡ = list items
- ✌️ = casual sign-off
- 🎯 = targeting or precision

Use sparingly. 2–4 emojis per post maximum. Never mid-sentence.

**Arrow lists for quick-fire items:**

> -> SaaS founders
> -> B2B agency owners

**Dash lists for steps or features:**

> - Intent-Based Outreach
> - Clay Outreach Systems
> - Identifying Sales Triggers

**Separator lines:** `----` or `---` to divide content sections from CTA. Not used within the body itself.

**Bold:** Used very sparingly — typically for a product name, a headline keyword, or a CTA instruction. Not for emphasis on ordinary words.

**No hashtag spam:** 2–3 hashtags at the very end, on their own line, all lowercase. Never in the body of the post.

## Tone markers

**She does not hedge.** Suprava makes direct claims. She doesn't say "this might work" or "in some cases." If something works, she says it works.

**She does not over-explain.** She trusts the reader to follow along. Setup is one paragraph, then she moves.

**She is not modest about results.** She states outcomes clearly: "This email copy got us 100+ sales calls." No qualification, no downplaying.

**She does not moralize.** She gives tactics and systems, not mindset lessons. When she does share a lesson (Template 16 on cold DMs), it's grounded in a specific stat or experience.

**She is not formal.** She uses contractions, fragments, and casual register throughout. "Not gonna lie" fits her voice. "Nevertheless" does not.

**She is not motivational.** She doesn't use inspirational language. "Success is a journey" is not her voice. "This strategy is best for SaaS founders who want 20-30 leads monthly" is.

---

## What to avoid

- Long paragraphs (more than 3 lines before a break)
- Vague outcomes ("improved results", "better performance", "great ROI")
- Hedging ("might", "could potentially", "in some cases")
- Motivational poster language ("believe in yourself", "the journey matters")
- Over-polished professional tone ("leveraging synergies", "driving outcomes")
- Thought-leader preambles ("In today's competitive landscape...")
- Em dashes used as literary punctuation — she uses line breaks instead
- Oxford comma (she doesn't use it)
- Multiple CTAs in one post

## Adapting the voice to new topics

Suprava's content is moving toward human-touch storytelling, creative GTM, and offline tactics (see `references/content-themes.md`). The voice guidelines above apply to all new content regardless of topic.

The structural patterns transfer directly. A post about a company that sent handwritten notes instead of cold emails uses the same hook patterns, the same step structure, the same CTA formats as a post about Clay workflows. The topic changes; the voice doesn't.

**Example of voice applied to a new theme:**

Old topic (cold email):
> You get 0 responses from your cold-email campaign because..
> You are still relying on Apollo.io's data!

Same voice, new theme (physical outreach):
> You are getting ignored in inboxes because..
> You are still sending emails when everyone else is too.

The hook structure, the accusation pattern, and the "you are still doing X" format are identical. Only the subject changed.
