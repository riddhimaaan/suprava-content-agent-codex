# LinkedIn Hook Library

The hook is the only line that decides whether the post lives or dies. It's the first 1–2 lines visible before "see more" — everything else is invisible until it earns the click.

Every post follows the STAR framework. The hook sits at the very top of the Situation beat. It must work as a standalone line — if the reader sees nothing else, the hook still has to land.

---

## How to Use This Library

1. Read the research brief and identify the core tension — the problem, the win, the counterintuitive lesson, or the data point that makes this story worth telling
2. Match that tension to one of the 18 hook types below using the Quick Selection Guide at the bottom
3. Write 2–3 variations using the hook's format
4. Pick the sharpest one — the one that earns the read without giving everything away
5. The hook becomes the opening line of the Situation beat in the post

**Selection rule:** Match the hook type to the emotional angle of the post, not the safest-sounding option. A curiosity hook on a tactical post is weak. A command hook on a personal story feels off.

---

## Hook Rules

- No warmup sentences before the hook ("In this post I want to talk about...")
- No softening language ("You might have heard that...", "Many people think...")
- No em dashes (— or –) — banned entirely
- One strong claim or question per hook — not two competing ideas
- Specific always beats vague: "7 meetings from 1 post" beats "great results"
- The hook is a promise — the post must deliver on exactly what the hook implies

---

## Style Principles

- **Concise and direct** — every word earns its place; cut anything that doesn't add force
- **Address the pain point immediately** — the reader should recognize their problem in the first line
- **Use numbers and specifics** — "7 meetings", "40% reply rate", "3 founders", not "many" or "significant"
- **Trigger words that signal value:** "How to," "Why," "Stop," "Never," "The one thing," "What most," "Here's what"
- **Lead with the benefit or outcome** — readers should understand what they get before they read on
- **One emotional trigger per hook:** FOMO, urgency, curiosity, fear of error — pick one and commit

## The 18 Hook Types

---

### 1. Urgency-Based
Conveys a time-sensitive problem or closing window. Reader feels like action is required now.

**Format:** [Action word or situation] + [time frame] + [specific outcome at stake]

**Examples:**
- "Most founders kill their GTM before Q4 even starts. Here's what to fix this week."
- "The window to build a content-led pipeline before year-end is 6 weeks. Here's the play."
- "Q1 pipeline gets built in October. Most B2B teams don't start until January."

---

### 2. Curiosity-Driven
Makes the reader wonder what they're missing. Withholds just enough to force the scroll-stop.

**Format:** [Surprising stat or fact] — [implied gap or question that goes unanswered]

**Examples:**
- "80% of B2B cold emails get ignored — and it's not the subject line."
- "3 founders pitched the same idea last quarter. One closed $200K. The difference wasn't the deck."
- "The highest-performing piece of content we've seen this year had 47 words."

---

### 3. Outcome-Oriented
Leads with a specific, desirable result. Reader wants that outcome and reads to find out how.

**Format:** [Specific measurable result] + [timeframe] + [minimal or unexpected input]

**Examples:**
- "7 booked meetings from 1 LinkedIn post. No ads. No DMs. Here's the setup."
- "We doubled a founder's inbound pipeline in 6 weeks without changing the offer."
- "One content series replaced an entire outbound motion for a B2B startup. Here's what was in it."

---

### 4. Problem-Solving
Names the exact pain point first. Readers who have that problem stop scrolling immediately.

**Format:** [Relatable pain point framed honestly] + [direct signal that a solution follows]

**Examples:**
- "Struggling to convert content followers into pipeline? The funnel is broken in one specific place."
- "Your GTM strategy isn't failing because of the product. It's failing because of this."
- "If your content gets likes but no DMs, the hook is fine. The CTA is the problem."

---

### 5. List / Number
Promises multiple specific insights in a scannable format. Sets clear expectations before the read.

**Format:** [Number] + [specific thing] + [implied benefit or outcome]

**Examples:**
- "5 GTM moves that closed deals without a single sales call."
- "3 cold outreach mistakes that killed reply rates — and what replaced them."
- "7 things I'd change about our first 90 days of content if we were starting over."

---

### 6. Fear-Based (FOMO / Mistake Avoidance)
Uses fear of a costly mistake or a missed window to compel attention. Reader slows down because they're worried.

**Format:** [Costly mistake or missed window] + [consequence or cost of ignoring it]

**Examples:**
- "If your content strategy doesn't include a distribution layer, you're building in a vacuum."
- "Avoid these 4 GTM decisions that waste the first 90 days of a new market entry."
- "Most B2B founders make the same content mistake. It costs them 6 months of pipeline."

---

### 7. Bold Claim
Makes a single, confident, undiluted statement. No hedging. Forces the reader to agree or push back.

**Format:** [Strong declarative claim] — stated flatly, no qualifiers

**Examples:**
- "Most B2B content fails before it's written."
- "Cold outreach isn't dead. Bad cold outreach is."
- "Your ICP is probably wrong. And your content is aimed at the wrong person because of it."

---

### 8. Question
Poses a direct question that hits the reader's exact pain point or aspiration. Reader answers it in their head and keeps reading.

**Format:** [Direct, pointed question that names the problem or the gap]

**Examples:**
- "What's the difference between content that builds pipeline and content that just gets likes?"
- "Are you optimizing for followers or for conversations?"
- "How many inbound leads did your last 10 posts generate?"

---

### 9. Teaser
Offers just enough to create intrigue — withholds the key detail so the reader has to click through to get it.

**Format:** [Hint at the insight or story] + [signal that there's something the reader doesn't know yet]

**Examples:**
- "The best-performing GTM campaign I've seen this year didn't start with a product."
- "What top-performing B2B content creators do differently — it's not what most people teach."
- "One hiring decision changed how this startup generates pipeline. It wasn't a sales hire."

---

### 10. Command
Directly tells the reader what to stop or start doing. Authority tone. Creates urgency through directness.

**Format:** [Stop/Start/Do] + [specific action] + [implicit cost of not doing it]

**Examples:**
- "Stop writing content for your peers. Write for your buyers."
- "Start tracking content-to-pipeline, not content-to-follower-count."
- "Stop treating LinkedIn as a broadcast channel. It's a conversation channel."

---

### 11. Data-Driven
Leads with a specific stat, internal result, or measured outcome. Credibility before the reader processes a word.

**Format:** [Specific number, stat, or result] + [what it reveals or implies]

**Examples:**
- "40% reply rate on cold outreach — from a 5-email sequence. Here's what changed."
- "One LinkedIn post drove 12 inbound leads in 72 hours. The hook was the only difference."
- "We tracked 30 B2B content accounts for 90 days. The pattern was obvious."

---

### 12. Success Story
Opens with a tangible, real result. Reader thinks: I want that. Works best when the result is surprising relative to the input.

**Format:** [Real, specific result] + [context that makes it surprising or credible]

**Examples:**
- "How a founder went from 0 pipeline to 3 signed contracts using only LinkedIn content."
- "We took a B2B startup's content from 200 views to 14K in 90 days. No content team."
- "One founder closed her first 10 clients without a website. Here's how she built trust instead."

---

### 13. Negative / What You're Doing Wrong
Names an error or blind spot the reader probably has. Uncomfortable enough to stop the scroll.

**Format:** [What most people do] + [why it's wrong or what it costs]

**Examples:**
- "If you're treating LinkedIn like a billboard, you're doing GTM wrong."
- "You're not losing deals because of price. You're losing them at the first touchpoint."
- "Most B2B founders write content about their product. Their buyers don't care about the product."

---

### 14. Trend-Based
Connects to a real shift happening right now. Feels timely, urgent, and relevant to what the reader is navigating.

**Format:** [Current shift or trend] + [what it means for the reader right now]

**Examples:**
- "B2B buyers are doing 70% of their research before they talk to sales. Your content is that research."
- "Why every GTM team is adding a content function in 2025 — and most are building it wrong."
- "The way B2B buyers discover new vendors is changing. Most founders haven't adjusted yet."

---

### 15. Case Study / Real-World Example
Names a specific company, founder, or campaign. Anchors the post in proof, not theory.

**Format:** [Named person, company, or campaign] + [what they did] + [surprising result]

**Examples:**
- "One founder replaced a 5-person outbound team with a single content series. Here's what happened."
- "How a 3-person B2B startup out-GTM'd a funded competitor — without a sales deck."
- "This bootstrapped agency used physical mail to land its first 3 enterprise clients."

---

### 16. Fear of Loss
Makes the reader feel they are actively losing something right now — not in the future, but today.

**Format:** [Ongoing loss happening right now] + [the simple, avoidable thing causing it]

**Examples:**
- "Every week you publish without a distribution strategy is a week of content that dies in 48 hours."
- "You're losing warm leads to competitors who show up consistently in the feed. Your content doesn't."
- "Every post without a clear next step is a lead you walked away from."

---

### 17. Curiosity + Urgency Combination
Pairs something the reader doesn't know yet with a reason they need to know it now. Double pull.

**Format:** [Curiosity gap — something unknown or surprising] + [time pressure or window that's closing]

**Examples:**
- "This GTM shift is already underway — most founders won't notice until it's too late."
- "There's a content format outperforming everything on LinkedIn right now. Most people haven't touched it yet."
- "Something changed about how LinkedIn distributes content in the last 60 days. Here's what we found."

---

### 18. Controversial / Counterintuitive
Challenges a widely held belief in the industry. Invites debate. Forces the reader to take a position.

**Format:** [Widely accepted belief or conventional wisdom] + [direct challenge or inversion]

**Examples:**
- "Consistency isn't the most important thing in content. Distribution is."
- "Your best clients don't come from your best content. They come from your most specific content."
- "More followers won't fix a broken GTM. Better targeting will."

---

## Quick Selection Guide

| Post angle | Best hook types |
|---|---|
| Tactical how-to or step-by-step | 3 (Outcome), 5 (List), 10 (Command), 11 (Data) |
| Personal story or earned lesson | 2 (Curiosity), 12 (Success Story), 9 (Teaser) |
| Opinion or contrarian take | 7 (Bold Claim), 18 (Controversial), 8 (Question) |
| Research, data, or trend reveal | 11 (Data), 14 (Trend), 15 (Case Study) |
| Problem + solution post | 4 (Problem-Solving), 6 (Fear-Based), 16 (Fear of Loss) |
| Timely or urgency-driven post | 1 (Urgency), 17 (Curiosity + Urgency), 14 (Trend) |
| Mistake or blind spot post | 13 (Negative), 6 (Fear-Based), 16 (Fear of Loss) |
